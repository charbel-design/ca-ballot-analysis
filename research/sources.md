# Research Sources

Canonical sources for California ballot analysis, in priority order.

## Tier 1: Primary sources (authoritative)

### Candidate lists and ballot language
- **California Secretary of State**: sos.ca.gov — official candidate lists, certified 88 days before election
  - Candidate certification PDF: usually at elections.cdn.sos.ca.gov/statewide-elections/...
  - Ballot measure text and analyses
- **County Registrar of Voters**: each county has its own site (e.g., contracostavote.gov, sfelections.org)
  - Sample ballots, local measures, candidate statements, local candidate lists
- **California Fair Political Practices Commission (FPPC)**: fppc.ca.gov — campaign finance disclosures

### Vote records and legislative history
- **California Legislative Information**: leginfo.legislature.ca.gov — every bill, every vote, every author
- **CalAccess**: cal-access.sos.ca.gov — campaign finance (older system but comprehensive)

### District assignments
- **CA Secretary of State district lookup**: for confirming which districts a voter is in after redistricting
- **CA Citizens Redistricting Commission**: for map references (2025 Prop 50 maps in effect for 2026)

## Tier 2: Comprehensive reference

### Ballotpedia (ballotpedia.org)
- Most comprehensive candidate profiles
- Voting records and prior campaign history
- Ballot measure summaries with arguments for/against
- Endorsement tracking
- Caution: user-editable; verify important claims against primary sources

### CalMatters (calmatters.org)
- Nonpartisan explanatory journalism
- Voter guides for each election
- Deep policy coverage
- Best source for: historical context on measures, legislative dynamics

### League of Women Voters (vote411.org)
- Candidate questionnaire responses
- Nonpartisan by charter
- Best source for: direct candidate statements on specific issues

## Tier 3: Supplementary

### Major newspapers (local and statewide)
- LA Times, SF Chronicle, Sacramento Bee, Mercury News, San Diego Union-Tribune
- Endorsement editorials (useful as data points, not recommendations)
- Investigative coverage of candidates and measures

### Local/regional outlets
- Danville San Ramon, Pleasanton Weekly, Bay City News, Local News Matters
- Often the only source for county-level race coverage
- Filing deadlines, candidate lists, local measure details

### Candidate campaign sites
- Use for: stated positions, platform detail
- Do not use for: character assessment, opponent claims
- Always verify claims of accomplishments against primary sources

### Interest group endorsements
- California Teachers Association, California Chamber of Commerce, Sierra Club, Howard Jarvis, etc.
- Use as signal of who benefits from a candidate winning, not as recommendation
- Follow the money: endorsements usually align with PAC contributions

## Tier 4: Low-trust sources (verify before using)

- Wikipedia: okay for uncontested biographical facts, unreliable for recent/contested content
- Social media: direct candidate posts are primary sources; third-party claims are hearsay
- Partisan opinion sites: treat as advocacy, not reporting
- Aggregator sites: trace claims back to primary sources before citing

## Source verification rules

1. **Every factual claim in output must be traceable to a Tier 1 or Tier 2 source.** If you can only find a claim in Tier 3/4 sources, either find a better source or leave the claim out.

2. **Cross-verify contested claims.** If a claim about a candidate's record is surprising, find a second independent source before including it.

3. **Paraphrase, don't copy.** Copyright discipline per skill global rules. One short quote per source maximum.

4. **Date-check everything.** A candidate's 2018 position may not be their 2026 position. Prefer recent sources for current views.

5. **Flag uncertainty.** If research produces conflicting accounts, present the conflict rather than picking one.

## Research workflow

For each ballot item:

1. **Start at the official source.** Candidate list from SoS or county registrar.
2. **Get the basic profile.** Ballotpedia for each candidate — party, designation, prior offices, education.
3. **Build the policy picture.** Candidate website + Ballotpedia "positions" section + CalMatters voter guide + Vote411 questionnaire answers.
4. **Check the record.** If the candidate has held office, check votes and delivery claims against primary sources (leginfo.ca.gov for legislators).
5. **Check the money.** FPPC filings for major donors. Aligns with or contradicts stated positions?
6. **Check for disqualifiers.** Search candidate name + ["lawsuit", "charged", "resigned", "scandal"] to surface character gate concerns.
7. **Check endorsements.** Who's for this person? Who's against?

For ballot measures:

1. **Read the ballot language itself.** Check for court-ordered revisions (Measure B in CCC June 2026 is a live example).
2. **Read the legislative analyst / impartial analysis.** Usually in the voter information pamphlet.
3. **Find the measure's sponsor and opponent coalitions.** FPPC again.
4. **Check for historical analogs.** If it's a renewal, compare to the original. If it's a new tax, compare to similar past measures.
5. **Find the best arguments on both sides.** Read the official Yes and No arguments in the voter pamphlet, then look for more rigorous versions from policy groups.

## Red flags in sources

- **Vague attributions**: "Critics say..." without naming the critic
- **Outdated information**: A 2022 article about a 2026 campaign
- **Advocacy disguised as analysis**: Check for the organization's political orientation
- **Too-clean narratives**: If a candidate's record reads as unbroken success, you're reading their campaign material
- **Absence of opposing view**: Any source that doesn't acknowledge counterarguments is incomplete
