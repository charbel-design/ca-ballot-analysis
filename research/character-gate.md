# Character Gate Evaluation

## Purpose

The character gate is a binary filter applied BEFORE any weighted scoring. A candidate who fails the gate is disqualified regardless of how well their policies align with the framework.

This is the most important step in the analysis. Weighted scoring can produce close calls; the gate should produce clean calls when applied correctly.

## Gate principles

1. **A single incident is not a pattern.** One bad vote, one tone-deaf comment, one political misstep — not a gate failure. Humans make mistakes.

2. **A pattern is a pattern.** Repeated behavior that points the same direction IS a gate failure, even if each individual incident is debatable.

3. **Office relevance matters.** A pseudoscience belief is disqualifying for a scientific office; a personal religious practice is not disqualifying for most offices.

4. **Document the reasoning.** Always write out why a candidate failed the gate. This produces auditability and helps the voter see whether they agree.

5. **When in doubt, keep them in the pool.** Better to have a weak candidate score poorly in weighted criteria than to wrongly disqualify someone. The gate should be for clear failures, not borderline cases.

## Common gate conditions and how to evaluate them

### Rule-of-law violations

**What counts**:
- Ordering unauthorized government actions (seizures, arrests, confiscations)
- Refusing to certify lawful election results
- Calling for criminal prosecution of federal officers for executing statutory duties
- Advocating defiance of court orders
- Publicly attacking the legitimacy of judges who rule against them

**What doesn't count**:
- Policy disagreements with current law (normal politics)
- Legal challenges to statutes via proper channels (that's litigation, not lawlessness)
- Criticism of judges' reasoning (not the same as attacking their legitimacy)
- Running for office on changing the law (that's democracy)

**Evidence standard**: Direct quote or documented action. Hearsay doesn't meet the bar.

### Extremist organizational ties

**What counts**:
- Active or recent membership in organizations designated as extremist by nonpartisan monitors
- Financial contributions to extremist causes
- Speaking appearances at extremist gatherings
- Defense or minimization of extremist acts (sedition, political violence)

**What doesn't count**:
- Peripheral connections (attended one event 20 years ago)
- Views that are controversial but not extremist (controversial ≠ extremist)
- Association with mainstream political organizations that some consider extreme (partisan framing)

**Evidence standard**: Direct affiliation documented by nonpartisan sources. Opposition research claims don't meet the bar unless verified.

### Fundamental unseriousness

**What counts**:
- Campaign framing as religious war, cosmic battle, or spiritual struggle rather than governance
- Conspiracy theories as core platform pillars (not just occasional comments)
- Pseudoscience beliefs that affect the office's duties (an anti-vaccine pediatric health commissioner, a flat-earth transportation commissioner)
- Performative politics: running for office as a stunt or personal brand-building without substantive agenda

**What doesn't count**:
- Strong religious identity (most politicians have one; not disqualifying)
- Personal unconventional beliefs that don't affect office duties
- Provocative rhetoric (annoying, not disqualifying)
- Outsider status (non-traditional candidates can be perfectly qualified)

**Evidence standard**: Pattern across multiple statements, platform documents, or campaign material.

### Disqualifying judgment lapses

**What counts**:
- Criminal convictions related to office conduct (fraud, corruption, abuse of power)
- Pattern of documented lies about material facts (not just spin)
- Abuse of power in prior office (using official position for personal gain, retaliating against critics)
- Professional misconduct findings in regulated fields (bar discipline, medical license issues) if relevant to office

**What doesn't count**:
- Personal mistakes unrelated to office conduct
- Old offenses for which the candidate has demonstrably reformed
- Rhetorical exaggeration or normal campaign spin
- Unverified allegations (especially from opponents)

**Evidence standard**: Court records, licensing body findings, or investigative reporting with multiple sources.

## How to apply the gate

For each candidate:

1. **Collect red flags**: Search candidate name with terms like "lawsuit," "charged," "resigned," "controversy," "extremist," "criminal" using primary sources.

2. **Categorize each red flag**: Does it fit one of the gate conditions? What's the severity? Is it a pattern or an incident?

3. **Evaluate against standards above**: Does it meet the "what counts" bar? Does the "what doesn't count" list apply?

4. **Apply the doubt rule**: If you're genuinely uncertain, document the concern but keep the candidate in the pool. Let weighted scoring handle borderline cases.

5. **Write explicit gate-failure reasoning**: If disqualifying, produce 1-2 sentences stating exactly why, with source. This belongs in the final output.

## Sample gate-failure language

Good:
> "Candidate X fails the character gate. In 2025, while serving as Sheriff, X ordered deputies to seize ballots from drop boxes over unfounded election integrity concerns, drawing a court order to return them. This is a direct rule-of-law violation, not a policy disagreement."

Good:
> "Candidate Y fails the character gate based on a consistent pattern of religious-war framing in campaign materials, including campaign literature describing the election as 'a battle between light and darkness' and debate appearances centered on stopping 'grooming.' This is unseriousness as core messaging, not incidental rhetoric."

Bad:
> "Candidate Z fails the character gate because they hold views I disagree with." ← Policy disagreement is not a gate.

Bad:
> "Candidate W fails the character gate because their opponent accused them of corruption." ← Unverified allegation.

## Output format for gate failures

In the final report, present gate failures in a dedicated section, not buried in the candidate analysis. Structure:

```
## Candidates who failed the character gate

### [Candidate Name] ([Office])
- **Gate condition failed**: [Category]
- **Evidence**: [1-2 sentences with sources]
- **Why this is disqualifying, not just negative**: [Why this isn't just a policy disagreement]
```

This makes the gate auditable. A voter who disagrees with a gate call can point to the exact reasoning and push back.

## When to revisit gate calls

The gate isn't static. If new evidence emerges:
- A claimed gate failure turns out to be unsubstantiated → remove the failure, re-score
- New evidence of gate-level conduct surfaces → add the failure, disqualify

Always date-stamp gate calls so readers know the state of evidence at the time of analysis.
