# Framework: Common-Sense Fairness (Not Equity)

## Overview

This framework scores candidates and measures on whether they advance common-sense fairness: rules applied evenly, individual merit over group identity, opportunity over redistributed outcomes, and measurable improvements to Californians' lives that survive a 5-year horizon test. It rejects "equity" as a normative goal when equity means unequal treatment to produce equal outcomes.

This framework is a value choice. Reasonable people reject it. Use this framework only if it matches your actual priorities.

## Character gate (binary pass/fail)

A candidate who fails any gate condition is disqualified regardless of policy alignment. No partial credit.

Gate conditions (any one disqualifies):

- **Rule-of-law violations.** Documented actions that undermine the rule of law: ordering unauthorized seizures, refusing to certify lawful elections, calling for criminal prosecution of federal officers performing statutory duties, etc.
- **Extremist organizational ties.** Past or present affiliation with organizations that have endorsed political violence, sedition, or racial/religious supremacy.
- **Fundamental unseriousness.** Religious-war rhetoric as a governance frame. Pseudoscience beliefs affecting the office. Conspiracy theories as campaign pillars.
- **Disqualifying judgment lapses.** Criminal convictions related to office conduct, documented abuse of power, pattern of demonstrable lies.

A single incident is not automatic disqualification; a pattern is. Use judgment. If uncertain, document the concern but leave the candidate in the scoring pool with a character note.

## Weighted criteria (total 100%)

Score each remaining candidate 0-100 on each criterion. Apply weights. Apply feasibility multiplier where indicated.

### 20%: Delivery track record

Has this person actually run something at scale and produced measurable results? Legislative voting records without executive delivery experience score lower. "Advocated for" and "co-sponsored" score lower than "implemented" and "delivered."

Evidence: prior office outcomes, private-sector track record, measurable metrics (budgets balanced, crime rates, graduation rates, permit times, response times).

### 35%: Policy quality × feasibility

**This is a multiplicative criterion, not additive.** A candidate with excellent policies and near-zero feasibility scores lower than a candidate with good policies and realistic feasibility.

**Policy quality** (0-100): Does the policy address the actual problem? Does it pass basic economic logic? Does it avoid predictable unintended consequences? Does it respect constitutional limits and individual rights?

**Feasibility** (0-100): Can this candidate actually pass this agenda given their coalition, party alignment with the Legislature, and political reality? A Republican's ambitious statewide agenda in a D-supermajority state has low feasibility regardless of its merits.

**Score** = (Policy quality × Feasibility) / 100

### 15%: Second-order effects (5-year test)

Does this policy still look fair in five years? Policies that sound fair on day one and fail by year five are penalized. Rent control is the classic case: reduces supply, entrenches incumbents, worsens housing. Tax-funded backfills are another: they postpone structural reform and set up the next ask.

Evidence: historical analogs, supply-demand implications, incentive effects, precedent in other jurisdictions.

### 15%: Concentrated-benefit vs. diffuse-cost alignment

Organized groups (public employee unions, connected developers, protected industries) reliably win policy fights over disorganized taxpayers and consumers. This criterion favors candidates who explicitly fight concentrated-benefit capture, and penalizes candidates funded primarily by or aligned with such groups.

Evidence: campaign finance disclosures, voting record on union contract approvals, positions on regulatory capture, willingness to take on sacred cows.

**Authored-pork sub-test (heavy weight within this criterion).** For candidates with a legislative record, check authored or co-authored bills for earmarks, carve-outs, or concentrated-benefit provisions inconsistent with their stated platform. A fiscal-discipline candidate who co-authored a union-specific carve-out, or a reform candidate who authored an industry-specific tax credit, should score materially lower than a candidate whose authored record matches their stated platform. This is the hypocrisy test: actions vs. words at the bill level, not just at the platform level.

Evidence: leginfo.ca.gov for authored bills, floor analyses identifying carve-outs, follow-up reporting on who benefits from specific provisions.

### 10%: Individualism vs. rule-equality resolution

When respect for individual choice and treating everyone the same conflict, how does the candidate resolve it? A well-specified framework handles this tension explicitly rather than ignoring it. Score based on whether the candidate's positions reflect a consistent principle or ad-hoc selection.

### 5%: Baseline competence

Literacy in the domain of the office. Minimum threshold, not a ceiling. A candidate can max out other criteria and still lose to a competitor who has basic fluency in the office they're seeking.

## Confidence calibration

After scoring, assign confidence:

- **High**: Composite score gap of 15+ points between winner and runner-up. Strong evidence on all six criteria. No major information gaps.
- **Medium**: Score gap of 5-15 points. Some evidence gaps but framework points clearly. Runner-up has plausible claim.
- **Low**: Score gap under 5 points. Thin research surface. Framework outputs ambiguous. Pick is essentially symbolic or tactical.

## Ballot measures

For ballot measures, apply criteria 15, 15, 10, and a substituted "does this advance or retard the fairness framework" criterion in place of the two candidate-specific criteria (track record, baseline competence).

Specific tests for measures:
- Does it redistribute from diffuse taxpayers to concentrated interests? (If yes → against)
- Does it create a new permanent spending obligation or tax? (If yes → high bar to clear)
- Does it restrict individual liberty (speech, property, movement)? (If yes → against, high bar to clear)
- Does it clean up existing bad policy? (If yes → in favor, lower bar)
- Does the ballot language appear honest and non-argumentative? (If no → skeptical)
- Who funds the Yes and No campaigns? (Follow the money)

## Known framework limitations

This framework will reliably:
- Favor candidates with executive delivery records over pure legislators
- Favor policies with rigorous 5-year analyses over politically expedient ones
- Disfavor candidates funded primarily by public employee unions
- Disfavor sales taxes, parcel taxes, and other regressive revenue tools
- Favor candidates who challenge regulatory capture

This framework will produce uncomfortable results for anyone who also values:
- Historical redress of structural inequities
- Group-level statistical parity as a policy goal
- Expansion of public-sector employment
- Stronger collective bargaining protections
- Precautionary principle on environmental policy

If those values are important to you, use a different framework.

## Signature picks this framework produces

As a calibration aid, this framework reliably produces picks like:

- Candidates with private-sector audit experience for Controller-type offices
- Industry-experienced candidates for regulatory offices (Insurance, etc.)
- Charter school and school choice advocates for education offices
- Housing-supply candidates (YIMBY-adjacent) over rent control advocates
- NO on most new sales taxes and parcel taxes
- YES on growth boundaries that force infill (second-order reasoning)
- Abstention on judicial races with insufficient public record

If your framework produces a different mix than this on actual CA ballots, you're using a different framework — that's fine, just name it.
